Researcher Domain[GA1] [AG2] [AG3] [RS4] [YL5] -Expertise Integration Pipeline

The document attempts to describe how we infuse human expert knowledge to our eval and FT pipelines to bridge the gap between generalized LLM outputs and precision needs of domain-specific workflows. The approach aligns with other AI leaders (including OAI, Anthropic) in how they leverage domain-specialist data and rigorously defined evaluation rubrics to systematically adapt and fine-tune foundational models for specialized tasks.

Pipeline

Here are the following steps our pipeline will follow:

Vertical Selection

Our vertical selection framework involves prioritizing scenarios for S500 customers with strong M365 Copilot adoption. We score each task on complexity of fragmented unstructured data involved in the task, business impact, standardization of output expectations, and domain data availability, as discussed in detail in Personas for Researcher Criteria for workflow selection.docx. Workflows for Corporate finance professionals and Legal professionals emerged as top opportunity areas. Within this scope, initially we are focused on M&A due diligence, 10-K/Q filings analysis, earning call prep reports, competitive intelligence for finance domain and patent prior art search & regulatory intelligence for legal domain. For the Mercor pilot we are scoping to Finance workflows like M&A due diligence, 10-K/Q filings analysis (may be refined after Mercor shares survey results)

 

Expert Data Collection (Mercors Contribution)

Domain experts (through Mercor) prepare the dataset for each task, which includes:

      Prompts (tasks): We expect 30 distinct prompts per workflow[SL6] [AG7] , which we believe should be sufficient for finetuning for a task. These prompts should be complex and resemble what a professional in that field might ask.

      Collection of evidence documents: Alongside each prompt, Mercor will provide domain specific input source documents[AS8] [AG9] [AS10]  (anywhere from 1 [RS11] [AG12] to 10 documents) required to answer it e.g. public filings, companys 10-K annual report, a financial spreadsheet, a news article, or an invention description. We should ask them if they can also generate relevant hypothetical enterprise data. In real use, Researcher agent might need to search this internal data, and hence we may need to synthetically generate this complexity in our corpus (more on this in the next sub-section). Based on the task, we may need database dumps besides self-contained files, in an easily parseable form so that our FT pipelines can ingest them using mock tools.

      Evaluation rubrics for each task: This is the centerpiece of the deliverable. The rubric should enumerate the criteria (from 5 to over 50 lines) an output is judged against. The sample JSON can be found here IB Example 1 Prompt_Rubric (777).json, listing the following for each criterion:

o   Description (e.g. Mentions JetBlues CFO by name)

o   Sources

o   Justification

o   Weight (Critical vs Major vs Minor).

o   Dependencies [SL13] [AG14] (if one criterion logically depends on another)

We would like this rubric to act as a checklist of quality criteria that AI-generated output should meet, along with our horizontal ACRUEP metrics. This includes content-expectations (e.g. Correctly calculates that the private Aerospace/Defense industry trades at 723.53%, + / - 4%), but we would like Mercor to include presentation/style metrics (e.g. tone, formality, structure, length) specific to that vertical as well (More on this later in the document).

      Golden outputs (expert answers): For each prompt, we can also ask Mercors domain experts to produce the ideal answer. Comparing AI outputs to this golden answer can give qualitative analysis, while clarifying the rubric.

 

Integrate into Pipeline and Build Eval Sets

Mercors prompts and documents are stored for the Researcher agent to execute against them. We leverage their scripts to perform LLM based evaluation score_output(output, rubric_set) to assess models output against the set of rubrics, similar to GroundLeo like LLM based metrics. At the moment, the rubric is graded in a single shot per output but there is opportunity to experiment with grading single criteria at a time or sampling multiple evaluations per criterion to gain more certainty.

One important question here is the grounding data. Todays synthetic data pipelines generate synthetic data, and the run horizontal or domain specific Researcher prompts on the synthetic tenant. Here given the (input, output) built by Mercor, we will try two approaches:

      One alternative is to deactivate the search tools and provide the supplied documents as context[RS15] [AG16] [RS17] [JC18] [AG19] . This version simplifies the task to optimize responding based on relevant information. Post-fine-tuning, the agent should learn output expectations but may also learn content to look for and may try to use the tools to fetch the relevant content. We have started to run tests against the sample Mercor data to evaluate effectiveness of this hypothesis.

      An alternate version that we have started to work upon is building a trail of communications around and leading to the input files in the synthetic tenant. This can also involve chunking the information in the input files into several different items. This will also allow us to introduce noisy data (conflicting information, duplicated information, etc.) which may present a more realistic environment during training.

Besides automated scoring using rubrics, we can also ask domain experts to periodically review outputs to provide qualitative feedback. They might catch nuances and the manual loop can ensure the rubric remains aligned with human judgment.

 

Fine-Tune Model

Given we have explicit criteria, we can use the rubric evaluation function to score model output. We use a training algorithm which introduces policy optimization to generates better CoTs based on the rubric feedback and do it in sample-efficient way, taking advantage of the off-policy capability where successful trajectories from historical policies are re-used. We train a generative model that estimates probability of CoT for a given user query and probability of the answer for the given (user query, CoT) pairs[LD20] .

For project status, we built generic rubrics representing a class of tasks vs Mercors rubrics are individualized for each prompt. We believe this is acceptable and may even be advantageous in capturing nuances of each scenario. We are actively building a variant of our pipeline to handle this approach easily. We will also consider combinations of the above, similar to the approaches observed by OAI.

Should there be a need for more data or variation, we can also consider either requesting or building ourselves counterfactual examples of a given data point, where the prompt and / or documents are adjusted, along with changes in the resulting assertions. These variations could be a way to challenge the model further and learn more effectively.

Evaluate Improvements

We will hold out a difficult portion of Mercors dataset (say 5 out of 30) for validation. If this does not prove to be enough data, we can either adjust the requirements with Mercor going forward, or leverage techniques we have built to synthesize additional data points following the shape of exemplars.

After training, we run the model on this subset and measure its performance using the same rubrics. We will also do qualitative checks to ensure the outputs are genuinely improved and not just gaming the rubric in a trivial way. We will also run the eval on the horizontal Researcher set to ensure no major degradation.

Early Adopter Feedback

We will periodically expose the promising improvements to real users in a controlled way. This ensures we havent overoptimized our test set and help test the model on actual use-cases. This would be through:

      Early adopters from S500 customers. CPM team has identified customers across FSI (BlackRock, Visa, JPM, Goldman) and Healthcare (Eli Lily, J&J, Mckesson) as some initial customers we can work closely with.

      Select internal MSIT teams e.g. Finance Data and Experiences Group (part of CFO org) and IPG Patents Group (part of CELA org)

Based on user feedback, we will make needed refinements and adjust the model further or update our rubrics to cover something we missed.

 

Refine & Scale:

The early adopter feedback will give us an informed go/no-go for scale. Positive feedback would be justification to ship the improvement as well as scaling the approach to cover more tasks or expand to another vertical domain. And negative would imply either pivoting our FT approach or possibly reconsidering the partnership. Either way, we will document the feedback carefully as evidence.

We will also need to integrate the process into our ongoing model development cycle, so future o3-DR updates continue to benefit from these domain optimizations.

The pipeline does not necessarily end after one iteration and either the whole pipeline or a subset of it would be looped back until we achieve a satisfactory level of performance and user satisfaction.

Necessary Modifications for Mercors Deliverables

A few modifications and clarifications to ensure their deliverables fit seamlessly into our pipeline and meet our evaluation goals:

      Mercors criterion are typically at the claim level, checking for specific facts or inclusions e.g. Ranks SpaceX as the private company with the highest selling interest. For a holistic evaluation, we need some criteria to be more aggregated, and also covering format, organization, and tone of the output. For example, contains at least 3 risks. We will ask Mercor to augment rubrics pertaining to:

o   Structure/sections: e.g. Has a conclusion section

o   Formatting: e.g. Bullet points are used over large paragraphs.

o   Tone and style: e.g. The report does not use casual language or emojis

o   Length or conciseness: e.g. Does not exceed 1000 words

      Allow multi-valued scoring: Mercors rubric line items are today binary checks, with an attached importance label (like Critical, Major, etc.). Some criteria can benefit from a more graded evaluation. In our internal metrics for project status, we considered using continuous scores for things like sentiment or formality, as well as varied penalty for deviating from the mean. We will understand if Mercors experts can assign partial credit in this manner.

      Data formatting and schema alignment:

o   Rubrics in their JSON format should work.

o   Prompts and Golden Answers in a JSON

o   Documents in folders named by PromptID, potentially with a manifest file listing document names per prompt.

By setting these expectations with Mercor and modifications to our pipeline, we de-risk the integration.

Key Risks and Considerations

Quality Risk

We need to ensure Mercor provides:

1.        Realistic and challenging prompts

2.        Rubrics must be precise and comprehensive.

We will measure these by validating tasks with our early adopters and rubrics through eval pipelines. If the eval score too high against rubrics, then the rubrics are not deep and comprehensive and largely only prevent catastrophic failures (like our LLM Asserts). If our eval scores are extremely low, we should verify if the rubrics are indeed correct, and why is Researcher not able to pass those.

To mitigate risk, we will detect data quality issues early to allow course corrections. A 4-week fixed-cost pilot limits our exposure but even further, Mercor delivers us an initial batch of 10 tasks in the first 1.5 weeks and we should leverage this pilot within a pilot to evaluate quality.

We will also explore other data vendors like Surge and Micro1 in parallel to understand the industry and its deliverables in general.

 

Pipeline Integration & Throughput Risk

If Mercor delivers faster than we can integrate and run our relevance processes E2E, we might have a huge backlog and wouldnt be able to give Mercor quick feedback. Our FT pipelines are still in their infancy, and optimizing relevance from complex, custom evaluation tasks is a tall order. Running and analyzing the results on new tasks will also require bandwidth. And there may also be bottlenecks on computing resources and approvals.

To mitigate, we will finish plumbing work and writing converters before the actual data arrives. We have started the dry run using 5 sample prompts they provided and are running eval on current Researcher and ChatGPT for those tasks. We are also in the process of making synthetic tenant changes, as well as executing our FT pipeline using specialized reward function based on the provided rubrics. The goal here was to test our end-to-end process (from loading a rubric to training with it). This would surface tooling issues, approvals and issues during analysis steps.

 

We are also recording fine-tuning iteration times to understand our bandwidth. This can help us raise needs for securing the necessary compute resources if we are lacking. We can also use this to set a contingency where we can reduce scope and still get a meaningful result on fewer tasks.

Model Fine-Tuning Risks (Overfitting or Low Impact)

There is an inherent risk that after fine-tuning, the model might either not improve enough on vertical tasks, or worse, it might overfit to the vertical and degrade on other queries.

1.        Minimal Model Gain: Its possible that Researcher LLM is already quite competent on these tasks and the additional data doesnt teach it much beyond its pre-training. Worse, OAI might have used similar tasks while training o3-DR in the first place. In that case, domain-specific metrics might just marginally improve. This risk ties to ROI where the money buys us little enhancement.

2.        Overfitting (Catastrophic forgetting): Fine-tuning on a narrow domain could cause the model to lose some of its generality. The impact would show as a regression in our non-vertical eval sets. Theres also a form of overfitting where the model performs well on the specific eval prompts but doesnt generalize to slightly different prompts in the same domain. That would mean our evaluation sets are not comprehensive and fully representative of real-world performance.

 

Mitigation: [DP21] [AG22] [LD23] 

1.        Baseline Evaluation and Success Criteria: Establishing clear baseline metrics before fine-tuning can help us learn scope of improvement. Comparing against ChatGPT and other competition can give us an idea on how much of the gap we currently have. The reward scaling may need tuning or trying adjustments like different learning rates or more examples. If nothing moves the needle, it might indicate diminishing returns of this approach for the given task.

2.        Regular Validation to Detect Overfitting: Before we take results to Early adopters, we will maintain a validation set of tasks that the model doesnt see during training. Additionally, we will verify fine-tuned models performance on general researcher eval sets the fine-tuned model to check for catastrophic forgetting. If needed, we can incorporate techniques such as regularization, or model head fine-tuning while freezing most weights so that the model retains prior knowledge. To expand validation sets, we may augment the Mercor data with synthetic data, like the techniques used by other fine-tuning efforts.

Early Adopter Testing & Feedback Risk

When we put the fine-tuned model in front of our early adopter customers, the feedback could be negative or lukewarm. This could be if issues we solved arent as important to them, or new issues that we ignored. Even when the task meets all rubric criteria, users might find that the analysis isnt what they needed has not significantly improved than before. Another risk is disengaged or wrong users (who dont deal with our precise use-cases) may not give us high quality feedback, leading to false confidence or missed problems.

Mitigation: Select early Adopter Customers and internal Teams who have shown high interest in the specific improvements and have validated the tasks we are working on. We will schedule periodic checkpoints during the pilot window to ensure they are continually engaged. Given high need for urgency, this will also help us iterate quickly on the observed issues.
